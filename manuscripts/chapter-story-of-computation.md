## Chapter 2: The Story of Computation

### Roadmapping the journey

An intricate path lies in front of us, one that strings so many great achievements, inventions and milestones. They all fall in a symphony of direct and indirect connections between great thinkers - some have continued the works of the ones before them, some arrived at similar breakthroughs independently. It seems special that complete strangers may share similar pursuits and be driven by the same search for hidden truths. The scale of this journey should be seen as one of humanity's itself. Forgetting for a second any specific name, this story has the most sense as a quest larger than any one man alone.

We can look at this journey as multiple interconnected threads. Practical solutions were always a result of the way societies functioned in their own time and place. Great ideas usually come by looking at immediate problems. We shall mark the threads in our story belonging to the practical aspects as such. Even though there's no rigid distinction, we shall similarly note the more theoretical parts of our journey.

It is also with great interest that we shall look at the more abstract discoveries. They share an elegance that seems more to belong to the Universe's order, rather than to mankind. It is why we usually say that mathematics and sciences are discovered rather than invented. Theory has the elegant appeal of being grounded not on immediate gain but on thoughts that never cease to bother the mind of the thinker.

### The (very) early beginnings

Way back before computers, the Industrial Revolution or even before agriculture and the first villages - before history itself, we might say, we, humans, realised that our strongest asset is our reasoning. We have discovered that abstract ideas can create practical solutions and that the chaos of the world around us can be understood through the structure of thought.

Imagine being a tribesman, the so-called hunter-gatherers, who spent their day roaming through thick and dangerous forests, in search of food and shelter. We have not yet developed settlements, let alone societal order or rules. Most importantly, we're clear from stressing about taxes, accounting and planning meetings. The only KPIs for now are survival, reproduction and food.

Our main logistical challenge consists of finding our way through intertwined trails. Some are familiar, some come with danger. One may lead to a welcoming valley whose river is rich in fish. The other may cross steep rock-climbs. There's one that intersects paths with herds of animals, another where valuable forest fruit grows. All these trails have some sort of familiarity, our elders used to walk them and our offspring will walk them just the same. But there's no point in trying to find our way and plan ahead without some proper way of recognizing them.

So here's an idea: Let's use one of the pointy stones that we use for hunting to carve out something in trees along the way. And, crucially, at any crossroads, let's clearly assign a set of symbols so that we can distinguish between the trails. It's not a small thing to come up with this idea. A deliberate action that changes the environment so that the environment carries a message for our future selves is something relatively uncommon in the animal kingdom (counter-argument here is the territory-marking behavior seen in multiple species)

Fictional image of a tree carving. The top symbol represents the trail identifier, the wavy line represents a river crossing, the third represents animal paths and the bottom one represents a rocky climb

Why stop here though? Instead of "naming" the trails with symbols, let's also try to describe them - let's use symbols to mark out a list of _features_ of the trail: geography, resources, dangers. This is a much more general approach and allows us to directly address the crucial information without the need of passing any other context.

There's also one very important moment we shall not overlook. Archeologists discovered a baboon bone, dating back to around 20 000 BCE. What's so special about it? Well, it shows a pattern of notches made on it. They are deliberate markings that more plausibly than being simple decorations, indicate a counting mechanism. First and foremost, this is proof of the human mind beginning to generalize and understand abstractions.

What's counting after all? You cannot count things unless you accept a conceptual similarity between them. Rarely are the objects themselves identical. They are, technically speaking, unique, but we choose to consider them similar enough to be considered "the same". Intelligence manifests itself by the capability to juggle between treating objects as a group and treating objects as independent.

The Ishango Bone shows markings considered to represent counts of days or specific goods.

The moment we start looking at our environment as a set of precise, discrete features, is the moment when our thoughts begin to have structure. For the first time, parts of the world are being encoded into stable symbols that can be read, interpreted, and acted upon later, by someone else, in a different moment. This passover of information proves to be extremely valuable, as we are no longer bound by the limited experience one can gather by himself. Knowledge becomes embedded in physical forms and with that comes a need for our thinking to become much more complex.

### Our first rules (rather practical)

We can speculate on the first organised behaviours of tribesmen and how they showed the signs of early logical structures: a proto-logic, if you will. But the following period, antiquity, offers better proof than wild guesses. It is time to step forward to the times when people began forming permanent settlements. Around 10,000 BCE, agriculture became a thing, so much so, it became partially redundant to spend entire seasons hunting for your tribe. Why do that when you can grow wheat in your own backyard? This meant, at the same time, that societies began to form as more and more people gathered in resource-rich areas. Getting organised and thinking of efficient ways of doing things became a necessity. And with that humanity had to come up with smarter and smarter ways to look at the world around us.

Imagine now we're ancient Babylonians (~1700 BCE) and our days are governed no longer by sole instinct but by principles and rules. King Hammurabi has listed for his people commands on what is allowed and what's not - and more importantly, the consequences of not abiding by them. If a man shall steal from another man, then he shall give back tenfold ([cite](https://ehammurabi.org/law/8)). Or if a builder constructs a house for someone and the house collapses and the owner dies, then the builder shall be punished ([cite](https://ehammurabi.org/law/229)). Lo and behold, logical structures emerge as strings of assessments and implications. The construct of if…then…else statements allow us not only to consider the present state but address consequences and effects of these statements in the future. Isn't this an amazing abstraction capability?

All animals must have a way of understanding the future. Cats, dogs, fish, birds - all foresee and anticipate what would happen next. A bird flies a straight line and spots how it will eventually cross an obstacle, which would result in a crash. So it swerves before that happens. But humans do something different. We don't just foresee one outcome, we imagine many branches of possibilities. And these possibilities - and the actions that make the choice between them - are best formulated as if…then…else statements. So this is our next step in the chain of intelligent inventions: listing out possible outcomes as functions of certain pre-conditions.

Still in our Babylonian times, there's one key aspect which governs the well-being of everyday life: agriculture. And more precisely, irrigation. Rainfall is not sufficient and by themselves, the lands cannot sustain proper growing of cereals and vegetables at the scale needed for the entire population. So it's up to us to create the infrastructure that would turn dry land into land fertile for crops. There's water available, from the Tigris and Euphrates rivers - but there's a downside: its flow is not consistent. Its unpredictability is incompatible with our constant demand.

This frames the classical paradigm where human ingenuity flourished: bridging the gap between unpredictable supply and calculated result. Turning chaos into control. This is what spared us to not depend on the mercy of the context. To not solely be the product of our environment but make our environment a product of us - for better or worse. (yes, that's the line from The Departed).

Ok, so the "why" is clear. But what about the "how"? The answer is _algorithms_! An algorithm is what you get when you take a goal and derive a sequence of instructions to follow. These instructions are based on decisions that must be taken based on specific conditions. It's a plan of what to do, step by step and how to respond to variations from it. A well-designed algorithm ensures that for every situation, there is a defined, optimal action.

And this is exactly what enabled large-scale irrigation to come about.

We shall build canals and reservoirs to make use of the high waters of the river when they occur. If the crops have been watered, then raise the gate to fill the reservoir. If the water reaches a certain marker, then divert the flow to the southern fields. The northern fields shall be watered only if the reservoir is full and the southern fields have been already watered. If the level in the reservoir goes below the last marker, then stop the flow to any of the fields..

The elegance of the algorithm is that it encapsulates the intelligence of the system in of itself. There's no need for complicated decisions once the algorithm is elaborated. Any worker can follow its rules only by evaluating the simple true-or-false conditions. This, in turn, makes the whole system sustainable. It no longer depends on continuous oversight and decision-making. And knowledge and its application into concrete action becomes transferable and delegable.

### The building blocks of logic (it starts getting abstract)

Early mathematics did not begin as a quest for eternal truths but as a response to stubborn, earthly problems. In Mesopotamia, by the Old Babylonian period (c. 2000-1600 BCE), scribes were already deploying sophisticated numerical procedures to manage fields, canals, taxation, and trade. Their clay tablets read like carefully engineered recipes: given certain measurements, apply these steps to obtain the result. Egyptian practice followed a similar path. The Rhind Mathematical Papyrus preserves practical rules for computing areas and volumes, including an impressively accurate approximation for the area of a circle. Even the celebrated geometric precision of the pyramids reflects not abstract theory but the culmination of highly refined applied measurement. Geometry, at this stage, remained firmly tied to land, labor, and logistics.

The Greek world inherited this toolbox of practical techniques but gradually began to ask a different kind of question. Tradition credits Thales of Miletus (c. 624-546 BCE) with an early breakthrough: instead of merely applying geometric rules, he sought to show why certain relationships must always hold. The shift deepened with Pythagoras (c. 570-495 BCE) and his followers, who advanced the striking claim that number and proportion underlie the very fabric of reality. Geometry was no longer just about measuring plots of land; it was becoming a way of uncovering necessity itself.

This transformation reached its philosophical climax with Plato (427-347 BCE). For Plato, the perfect circle or triangle was not something one could ever draw in sand or carve in stone. Physical figures were merely imperfect shadows. The true objects of mathematics, he argued, inhabit an ideal realm accessible only to reason. With that move, geometry stepped decisively into abstract space, and mathematics became the study of necessary relations between ideal forms. Once thought itself was seen as something that could be structured with this same rigor, the next step became almost inevitable - and it is here that we arrive at Plato's student, Aristotle, who would attempt to formalize the very machinery of reasoning.

It was Aristotle (4th c. BCE) who started to formalize logic and consider the scientific study of reasoning. He envisioned that simple statements can act as truth-bearing descriptors. We can then use these statements as pieces to assemble our arguments from, so that the arguments become objective and self-sufficient. Logical arguments shall not be subjective or depend on the charisma of their discourse. Rather, they shall be built on a rigorous structure that tells us how to arrive at a conclusion based on prior assumptions. Aristotle called this a Syllogism:

_If all A are B and all B are C, then all A are C_ ([cite](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/prior.2.ii.html)).

Or, if you prefer substituting A, B and C for arbitrary concepts:

_All humans are mortal. Socrates is a human. Therefore Socrates is mortal._

A century later, the Stoics extended and enriched these ideas of formalised logic. They noted that some assumptions can be optional, meaning that either one being true is sufficient. In other cases, all assumptions had to be true or else we wouldn't have our conclusion. (Funny thing is, this paragraph itself is very hard to write without using the exact same logical operators it discusses).

They arrived at a list of operators which would make up for any of these scenarios. Arranging them in specific sequences and in specific relations to one another yields a complete reasoning system. They outlined the fundamental building blocks of computational logic.

_IF-THEN, AND, OR, NOT_

_I don't want to spoil the story, but this moment is not to be underestimated. You may say - what's this got to do with modern-day computers? Why waste time on something that feels so obvious that it seems not worth noting down. But time is not wasted! See, these simple constructs act like neat brickwork. No individual brick seems too important. But arranged in order, bricks shape up walls and walls shape up the building. Similarly, we will see, these simple constructs are what's needed to assemble the entire logical system of any computer, down to its core components. Computers are a gigantic assembly of true and false values, assigned by combinations of only AND, OR and NOT. But let's not jump ahead._

### The first calculator (clearly practical)

Between Aristotle's formal logic and the whirring gears of early calculators lies a long and easily overlooked chapter of intellectual consolidation. Greek mathematics did not vanish with antiquity; it was systematized most famously by Euclid (c. 300 BCE), whose _Elements_ turned geometry into a model of deductive certainty that would dominate mathematical thinking for nearly two thousand years. The Greeks had shown that reasoning itself could be structured, but for many centuries the primary task was preservation, commentary, and gradual extension rather than radical reinvention. Knowledge moved east and south - into the Hellenistic world, into the libraries of Alexandria, and later into the scholarly centers of the Islamic Golden Age - where mathematicians refined algebra, numerals, and astronomical computation.

By the late medieval period, Europe began to reabsorb this accumulated knowledge through translations and trade. The adoption of the Hindu-Arabic numeral system - with its positional notation and the crucial concept of zero - quietly transformed calculation from a specialist's craft into a scalable administrative tool. Commerce expanded, bookkeeping grew more demanding, navigation required ever more reliable tables, and states developed increasingly complex tax systems. In other words, the practical pressures that had once driven Babylonian scribes returned in amplified form. Arithmetic was no longer occasional; it was relentless.

This slow tightening of numerical demands set the stage for a decisive shift. If calculation had become repetitive labor, perhaps it could be mechanized. The intellectual groundwork - symbolic reasoning from the Greeks, efficient numerals from India and the Islamic world, and the growing bureaucratic appetite of early modern states - was finally in place. What remained was to embed arithmetic itself into matter. And it is here, in the middle of the seventeenth century, that we encounter the first true mechanical attempt to make calculation automatic.

It is the middle of the 17th century, and, much to no one's delight, taxes occupy an increasing share of daily work. Arithmetic is no longer a concern only for scholars; it has become an administrative necessity. As trade expands, so do tariffs, conversions, and tallies that must be computed reliably.

In the French city of Rouen, a certain Étienne Pascal works as a tax commissioner. His duties require him to spend long hours adding and subtracting columns of numbers. The calculations themselves are not complex, but they are repetitive, and repetition invites error. Alongside him, his young son of eighteen years old watches him work. He cannot help but wonder whether there's a better solution to this cumbersome task.

He thought of the gears of a clock and how their precise movements followed along without mistake. And he asked himself whether the same cogs turning to reveal the passing hours could also point to the digits that make up a mathematical result.

He comes up with a mechanical device, a box showing a row of metal wheels. Each wheel represents a single digit and is marked with increments from 0 to 9. Together, the wheels correspond to the units, tens, hundreds, and higher positions of a number. The arrangement is linear, just as digits are written on paper. Above these is a row of narrow windows each showing a similar set of digits. These make up the result of the operation we'll perform.

To enter a number, the operator rotates the wheels forward, just like on an old phone. The wheels move with a firm, deliberate resistance, each click corresponding to an exact increment.

The true ingenuity of the machine lies beneath the surface, in how the wheels are linked. Each wheel is connected to the next by a small mechanical trigger. When a wheel advances from 9 back to 0, it does not simply reset. Instead, it nudges the neighboring wheel by one position. This is the mechanical equivalent of the _carry_ we perform mentally in arithmetic.

In other words, the machine enforces the rule:

_When one digit overflows, increment the next digit automatically._

To add two numbers, the operator must dial the first one. The wheels advance until the number appears in the display. The second number is then dialed in the same manner. Because the wheels begin from the existing configuration rather than from zero, each movement increments the previous state. The final arrangement of the wheels shows the correct sum.

The young inventor's name was Blaise Pascal, now known as one of the most influential mathematicians and philosophers of his time. With this device, later called the _Pascaline,_ he demonstrated that arithmetic could be delegated to a machine, provided its rules were embedded directly into its physical structure.

But Pascal's device had a limitation. The machine could only perform additions and subtractions. Multiplication was done by repeated additions, which brought back the possibility of human mistake. It was the turn of another inventor to solve this a few decades later. His name was Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz and as we'll see our story gathers quite a momentum by his multiple contributions. \[[cite](https://youtu.be/E0pJST5mL3A?si=-q2478hiba5Q93Is)\]

The key innovation was adding a stepped drum which allowed a single rotation to add different amounts depending on its position. By shifting the carriage and repeating rotations, the machine could effectively perform multiplication as a controlled series of additions. The operator was no longer required to keep track of intermediate results. By the contributions of these two brilliant inventors, humanity has achieved its very first calculator.

Leibniz named his invention the Stepped Reckoner. Which is a wording that also reflected his deeper passion. He wondered whether human reasoning itself could be turned into chains of computation. Just like we use numbers and rules to calculate amounts, could we turn arguments into mathematical expressions?

"If controversies were to arise, there would be no more need of disputation between two philosophers than between two calculators. For it would suffice for them to take their pencils in their hands and to sit down at the abacus, and say to each other: Let us calculate." \[[cite](https://en.wikiquote.org/wiki/Gottfried_Leibniz), [cite](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/311456139_Leibniz's_Characteristica_Universalis_and_Calculus_Ratiocinator_Today)\]\[[cite](https://plato.stanford.edu/archives/fall2013/entries/leibniz)\]

He envisioned a universal language of symbols that can express concepts and knowledge. Then we would be able to design a mechanism that operates on these symbols so that reasoning itself can be expressed, proved and understood this way.

It's fascinating to follow his journey as Leibniz was first and foremost a metaphysician. His interests were in math and engineering just as much as they were in theology and philosophy \[[cite](https://plato.stanford.edu/archives/fall2013/entries/leibniz)\]. In fact he viewed God as the ultimate expression of universal logic and his quest for structuring the reasoning process was a spiritual one. And surprisingly as it may sound, this combination of both rigorous science and mystical esoteric beliefs, drove Leibniz to lay down incredibly powerful mathematical tools that would show up behind many of the modern day's engineering marvels.

_Leibniz searched for a way to break the world into fundamental components, much like one might try to assemble a dish out of a set of ingredients. But no chef, however skilled, can make the world fit such a neat classification. However, Leibniz was unknowingly working at what would become, in a way, a universal set of symbols out of which any information can be assembled._

### The 1s and 0s (abstract, for now)

Leibniz was fascinated by how complex systems can emerge from very basic building blocks. He aimed to discover what would be the most minimal basis for numerical systems. In doing so, he thought of the most elementary distinction someone can observe. He would later articulate this in metaphysical terms: that there is either a "something" or a "nothing", a quantity or no quantity. Before worrying about amounts and counting the number of "somethings", there is this simple principle.

He started to consider this as potentially a complete numerical system in of itself. This system would consist of only two possibilities, the something and the nothing: 1 and 0. But in order to be a well-defined numerical system, it has to support all known arithmetic just like the traditional number system. Can this be the case? Why not - after all, didn't we arbitrarily choose 10 digits just because it so happened to match the number of our fingers?

So he set out to corroborate the equivalence between all our usual numbers mathematics to this new system. And he found out that the implications maintain an elegant symmetry.

First of all, how are we to represent quantities using nothing but 1s and 0s? Any mathematics requires us to work with numbers, from counting a few apples to summing the values in an accountant's ledger. Whatever system we choose must be able to express arbitrary quantities. Can we do this with only using two digits?

Well, in our regular number system that makes use of the digits 0, 1, 2, 3, up to 9, we can still represent numbers like 2644, 94763 or 3632. The individual digits work like building blocks, arranged so that they carry specific meaning about the quantities they represent.

Let's take the random number 2437. The position of each digit is not arbitrary. The 2 means two thousands, the 4 means four hundreds and the 3 means 3 tens. Decomposing the number by this explicitly yields:

2437 = 2 \* 1000 + 4 \* 100 + 3 \* 10 + 7

We see that 1000, 100 and 10 are all powers of 10 - which is the number of digits that we have at our disposal (1000=10^3, 100=10^2). So we can rewrite as:

2437 = 2 \* 10^3 + 4 \* 10^2 + 3 \* 10^1 + 7 \* 10^0

We can use the same trick for numbers consisting only of two digits. Making their position increment the amount they represent. The leftmost digits represent increasing orders of magnitude. Now, since we are working with only two digits, each position corresponds to a power of 2 rather than a power of 10.

So, a random binary number such as 1101011 will be equal to:

1 \* 2^6 + 1 \* 2^5 + 0 \* 2^4 + 1 \* 2^3 + 0 \* 2^2 + 1 \* 2^1 + 1 \* 2^0 = 107

By a reverse procedure we can choose to express ourselves like this for any mundane quantity. Just don't go to the market asking for 1100 eggs instead of a dozen.

And just like we can add up the numbers in a ledger's column, or multiply a quantity by its price, the same operations work in a binary system just the same. That's the elegance of mathematics.

_If we as humans had been born with only 2 fingers at each hand, we'd have no problem to develop the same mathematics and calculate taxes and tariffs just the same as we did with 10 fingers._

Of course, this looks like a flex by an idle mathematician, creating problems where there were none. And we can agree that this idea was far ahead of its time when Leibniz proposed it. Because there was no practical utility in counting in this fashion. But as time will show us, this will become the main framework for representing information in the age of computers. Why? - Because the concepts of "something" or "nothing" give us the most simple approach at structuring our systems into clearly defined states.

_It would be for more than a century, in a completely different intellectual climate, that another thinker would arrive at a strikingly similar quest. His name was George Boole and it will naturally ring a bell to any technical reader of this book. His legacy lives so deeply embedded in all our digital systems that his name has become a denominator for the most basic operator in all computer programs: boolean values - values which can either be true or false, elegantly corresponding just as well to 1 and 0._

George Boole was a largely self-taught schoolteacher with an unusual obsession: he wanted to make reasoning precise. Logic, despite Aristotle's foundations, was still mostly verbal and philosophical.

In 1847 he introduced a novel way of thinking. Boole asked a radical question: what if logical statements could be treated like algebraic quantities?

A statement such as "this object is red" is either true or false. There is no third option. Boole realized that logical variables only ever take two values: in or out, yes or no, true or false.

He introduced symbols for logical propositions and defined operations on them. In his system:

_A × B meant "A and B are both true."  
A + B meant "A or B is true."  
1 meant "true."  
0 meant "false."_

Logical arguments could now be manipulated like equations. Conclusions followed from algebraic rules.

Boole had laid the groundwork for something extraordinary: a complete symbolic system in which reasoning itself could be carried out mechanically, step by step, with guaranteed correctness. We know now that this fits perfectly with the numerical system designed by Leibniz which extends the powers of such a representation into a general system.

_But for now, all these had to remain in the realm of theory. Their practicality would not shine until their calculations could be performed at scale and in an instant. And even before that, the world was yet to realize that logic could be made to work on machines._

### Programmable Machines

_Here's a challenge for you, dear reader:_ try to describe the pattern you see on a woven tapestry carpet. Pick any geometric or floral motif from these traditional masterpieces. Now try to describe it so clearly that someone else could draw it from your words alone. How long do you think it would take?

In the 1800s, woven patterns were all the fashion. Skilled craftsmen produced them one by one, with painstaking attention so as not to lose the design while weaving thread after thread. Looms helped, of course, but much still depended on the weaver's continuous concentration. Moreover, it was difficult to pass down a pattern from one person to another. Even for the same person to reproduce his work perfectly a second time was a challenging task.

But a Frenchman, Joseph Marie Jacquard, looked at the problem considering how a pattern could be transformed into a list of precise instructions that could be followed by the loom itself. His automated loom would end up foreshadowing a principle that later shows up everywhere in computing: a machine that behaves differently depending on the instructions you feed it

The process is as follows. The design pattern is drawn on a sheet lined out with a fine grid of squares, just like a math notebook. Inspecting each individual cell, all 'partial' squares are filled out so that there's no fuzzy indecision for the boundaries of the design.

\[[video explanation](https://youtu.be/K6NgMNvK52A?si=YtGzbtN2vCFM629X)\]

With the drawing complete, the grid can be copied onto stiff cards, punching out holes corresponding to each filled-out square. Each row of the pattern corresponds to a card, and each position on that row becomes a hole or no hole.A single design might require hundreds of cards, stacked in order. But once they exist, they can be fed into the loom and replayed whenever you want, in exactly the same sequence.

The beauty is in how the loom reads them. A hole allows a needle to pass through and engage a hook, lifting the corresponding thread. For the positions where the card is not punched out, the needle is stopped and the thread stays down. Row by row, the woven image emerges. Each card acts like a sheet of tiny yes-or-no prompts: "lift this thread, or don't."

The loom itself doesn't have to change. Feed it one stack of cards and you get one design; feed it a different stack and you get another. Jacquard's loom was programmable in the most literal sense. The craft of weaving had gained something new: repeatable instructions that could be stored, shared, and run. But the automatization was not so enthusiastically received.

_One might argue that artistic value lives partly in imperfection. Today, in the market of antique tapestry, the imperfections are often what makes the price go up. The mistakes the weaver made are attributes of the uniqueness of the result._

_By this argument, Jacquard's automatization comes off as an uncalled-for upgrade. And sure enough, Jacquard did face strong pushback for his invention. Many weavers at the time feared that mechanization would cost them their jobs and undermine their craft. Many weavers feared mechanization would cost them their jobs and hollow out their craft. History has a sense of irony here: today we live among machines so precise and so fast that cloth made on a Jacquard loom would in all rights pass as "hand-made"._

Several decades later, someone in a completely different field saw the same idea. Inspired by the carpet-weaving punchcards, a German-American statistician named Herman Holerith had the idea of automating his own line of work. He was dealing with census data: age, occupation, gender, and other categories that had to be recorded and counted. He realized that much of his problem could be reduced to the same kind of simple choices: yes or no, this category or that one.

He came up with a system where each questioned person would be represented by a card. Hollerith's solution was to give each person a card. For every relevant category, a hole could be punched in a standard position: this age bracket, that occupation, this region, and so on. Once the cards were standardized, they could be sorted and counted mechanically. The census stopped being handwriting in ledgers and became something closer to data you could process.

But Holerith's system is not useful if the punch cards cannot be read quickly. A mechanical counter could sum up their tally but there's so much that can be achieved by mechanical components alone. If only there would be a way to instantly read each individual punchcard. Luckily for him, in the meantime to his invention at the end of the nineteenth century, the missing puzzle piece was waiting on the ready: electricity!

### Electricity

For so long in our history we have observed with wonder a series of phenomena that, at the time, could only be attributed to magic and mystics. Lightnings in the sky were signs of Gods' anger. Scholars observed strange properties of materials that could move other objects without touching them. Yet, there was no rigorous study to tie all this together.

Imagine! People were using compasses, guiding themselves overseas by complete trust in these little contraptions that pointed north. This meant nothing less than life-or-death. All this, without them having a comprehensive explanation of how it worked!

It was known that some specific rocks were able to move metals. It was in the 1600s that an Englishman, William Gilbert published a rigorous study explaining these phenomena. He was a scholar in medicine but deeply involved with philosophical research. This combination yielded an interest in magnetism, practiced over decades of work. He was the one to claim that "Earth itself is a magnet", finally addressing how compasses work.

Yet, this is only half of the coin. There were other phenomena which were not explained by magnetic forces. Ancient Greeks had observed that if you rub a piece of amber with a cloth, it gains the property of attracting light feathers. We have also seen this if we rubbed a balloon and then played around with how it makes our hair raise. Gilbert coined the term _electricus_, from the Greek _elektron_ ("like amber").

From here, interest in this field rose, especially as, in the 1750s, we have Benjamin Franklin's famous kite experiment, demystifying in the most practical manner that electricity was the explanation behind lightning. Moreover, his demonstration foreshadowed the capability of harnessing the powers of electricity. But there was still a problem: we had no way of summoning these powers on demand, where and when we needed it.

The leaps forward (pun intended) came from some rather wild connections between distinct domains. Alessandro Volta was discussing and fervently arguing about frogs legs. Yes, indeed, frogs! Apparently, during dissections, scholars observed muscle contractions triggered by the touch of metal utensils. Even though the frog was no longer alive, its legs twitched as if it were..

Volta reasoned that the key for understanding this was in the utensils themselves. Only a specific combination of two different metals produced this effect. The frog was just a contact medium. So he replicated this in the laboratory. He placed zinc and copper and between them a salt-water soaked cloth. And by doing so, he created the first battery.

This made experiments much easier. The effects of electricity could now be studied more extensively. And by such experiments came another revelatory insight, explaining the connection between electricity and its half-brother, magnetism.

Inspired by the belief that natural forces work in a unified connection, Danish philosopher Hans Christian Ørsted is studying whether there's a link between electricity and magnetism. He experiments with a voltaic battery and passes an electrical current through a copper wire. Next to the wire he places a compass and sees that the needle twitches when he moves it closer. He concludes that an electric current also produces a magnetic field in the space surrounding it.

The little twitch of the compass makes another inventor wonder: If electricity can _cause_ magnetic motion, can it produce continuous motion? He comes up with a smart design. A permanent magnet stood upright in a container of mercury (used as a conductor). A wire was suspended so that its lower end dipped into the mercury. Finally, the wire was connected to a battery, allowing current to flow.

When the current passed through the wire, it began to rotate continuously around the magnet. At first glance, the device looks almost trivial. But its implications are not to be underestimated. Electricity can now produce mechanical work. This was the first prototype of an electric motor.

A decade later, he realizes that the reverse principle applies identically. A wired core, spinning in a magnetic field produces electricity. There's a beautiful symmetry here, and it makes mechanical motion and electrical energy link up in an interchangeable relationship. Energy itself becomes a resource that can be transformed from mechanical to electrical and back and transferred from one location to another.

Basically, physical movement can now be transferred across vast distances. We can make use of the flow of a stream of water to drive the rotation of a paddled wheel. The wheel is connected to this electrical setup that generates electricity. We can then lay down long metal wires, stretching for kilometers on end, so that we connect our generator to its twin counter-part, the motor. And this motor transforms electricity back into motion. _Electricity enables us to think big. Its seemingly instant speed makes our world's distances suddenly shrink down. With this instant messenger, we are no longer disconnected from what happens in the nearing town. We now have something that would take less than the blink of an eye to travel from here to there and back._

### The telegraph

The year is 1844. The US Congress watches in amazement how a message sent from Washington DC reaches Baltimore in a blink of an eye. For the first time, information travels without any physical paper being delivered. The courier is electricity and it's lightning-fast. The message reads prophetically: "_What hath God wrought?_" ("_Look at what God has allowed to happen._") ([cite](https://www.loc.gov/item/mcc.019/)).At one end of the line stands Alfred Vail; at the other, Samuel Morse. Between them is a simple machine that makes distance feel smaller than it has any right to: the telegraph.

This wasn't yet the age of bright streets and household wiring. Electricity still belonged mostly to workshops and laboratories, where people experimented with circuits that could turn electricity into heat or motion. One setup in particular mattered for our story: a small magnetized arm that snapped when current passed through a coil, producing a crisp click.

Now imagine the battery in one room, and the clicking arm in another. Nothing about the setup requires them to be near each other. Stretch the wire through a hallway, across a street, out to the next house. When the circuit is complete, the clicker sounds off. We might not see the battery from where we stand, but the sound of the clicker tells us the electrical current is flowing.

And now, here's the t: keep the circuit open most of the time. Let the clicker sit in silence. Then, suddenly, it clicks. Someone, somewhere, completed the circuit. You may not know who it was or why they did it, but you've learned something all the same: an action took place at the far end of the wire.

_One could say that it's not just current flowing from one point to the other but also information. A simple, most basic: "this happened!", a simple truth statement._

To make this useful, you don't want to connect and disconnect wires by hand each time. You want a simple break you can control. So, let's place a lever that opens the circuit and closes it again, with the same ease that we turn the light on and off in our homes. A sidenote here: engineers call it "closing" the circuit when it turns on, which sounds backwards at first. The idea is that current flows only when the loop of the electrical circuit is complete; break the loop and it stops.

Once you have a switch, one click can become many. Tap quickly: click-click-click. Hold it down: a longer buzz. Leave a pause, then tap again. It starts to feel like a secret knock on a door. But only now the door can be fifty miles away.

With a bit of agreement, you can build a small dictionary: one pattern means "all is well," another means "danger," another means "send help." For some purposes that's already revolutionary. But it comes with a big limitation. You can only communicate what you have already decided to define in advance. The weakness of fixed codes shows up exactly when you need communication the most. If something unforeseen happens, your clever dictionary has no words for it. A finite set of meaning assignments quickly runs out of scenarios that could be covered. We can only communicate as much as we foresaw to encode in the dictionary.

We need a general _alphabet_ of symbols, just like letters and digits. They don't necessarily mean anything by themselves. But when put together like puzzle pieces they can make out any information. Our entire history can be formulated as a long sequence of letters and numbers.

If you can transmit letters, you can transmit words; and if you can transmit words, you can transmit pretty much anything humans know how to express. Earlier systems could do this in principle, but not in practice. You'd go mad trying to spell out a paragraph with smoke. A wire, on the other hand, can carry long sequences of taps without asking for clear skies or a line of sight.

This is where Vail and Morse made their second invention. They took the alphabet and assigned each letter a pattern made from two kinds of signals: a short tap (dot) and a longer one (dash). Silence did the rest of the work. A brief pause separates the parts of a letter; a longer pause separates letters; longer still separates words.

So Vail and Morse brought us two things. There was the physical apparatus: the wire, the switch, the receiver that clicked in answer - the telegraph. And there was the agreement that made those clicks mean something: the Morse code. A machine, and a language for the machine.

Centuries earlier, Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz had imagined that reasoning could be transformed into calculation. Living in an age fractured by theological and philosophical disputes, he longed for a method that could end argument altogether. If truth could be expressed symbolically, and symbols could be manipulated according to rules, then disagreement might dissolve into procedure. "Let us calculate," he proposed - not merely as a mathematical suggestion, but as a philosophical program. He introduced binary notation and dreamed of a universal language of reasoning in which judgment would yield to method. In Leibniz's imagination, thinking itself began to resemble computation.

This vision remained speculative until the nineteenth century, when industrialization changed how Europeans understood action and agency. The rise of the factory demonstrated that complex human tasks could be decomposed into repeatable steps and executed by machines. The Jacquard loom, guided by punched cards, showed that symbolic instructions could control physical processes. Charles Babbage absorbed this lesson and extended it from weaving to calculation. His Analytical Engine was not merely a more powerful calculator but a programmable system, equipped with memory, a processor, and conditional control. It embodied the idea that a machine could follow abstract instructions rather than perform a single fixed task.

Yet Babbage's project was fundamentally architectural. He showed that a machine might execute rules, but he did not ask what rules were, or whether all reasoning could be reduced to them. That question emerged from a crisis that shook the foundations of mathematics at the end of the nineteenth century. Paradoxes in set theory suggested that logic itself might be unstable. Mathematics, long regarded as the bedrock of certainty, appeared suddenly fragile. If its foundations were insecure, then the entire edifice of modern science and engineering stood in doubt.

David Hilbert responded with a program of extraordinary ambition: to formalize all of mathematics and demonstrate that it was both complete and consistent. Mathematics, he proposed, should be reduced to symbolic manipulation governed by explicit rules. If this could be achieved, one might hope for a general procedure capable of deciding the truth or falsehood of any mathematical statement. This was the Entscheidungsproblem - the decision problem - and it transformed the nature of the inquiry. The question was no longer philosophical but procedural: does there exist a method, a finite sequence of steps, that can determine truth?

In 1931 Kurt Gödel shattered the hope that such a system could be complete. His incompleteness theorems showed that any sufficiently powerful formal system would contain true statements that could not be proven within it. Yet Gödel's proof carried an unintended implication. In order to demonstrate the limits of formal systems, he encoded logical statements as numbers and proofs as sequences of symbolic operations. Reasoning, in his hands, became something that could be represented and manipulated mechanically. He did not construct a machine, but he revealed that logical thought could be translated into structured symbol processing.

The stage was thus set for a new question: what exactly counts as an effective procedure? Alonzo Church approached this through the abstract formalism of lambda calculus, attempting to capture the notion of computability in symbolic terms. But his framework remained distant from the practical activity of calculation.

Turing's insight was to reverse the perspective. Rather than define computation through symbolic logic, he examined what a human computer - the clerks who performed calculations by hand - actually did. These workers followed explicit rules, operated step by step, and relied on limited memory. Their work was procedural, constrained, and mechanical in spirit even when performed by human minds. Turing distilled this activity into a simple model: a machine that reads symbols, writes symbols, moves through a finite set of states, and follows deterministic rules.

From this operational analysis emerged the Turing machine. It was not designed as a physical device but as a conceptual one - an abstraction of process itself. Its power lay in its generality. Turing realized that the instructions governing any such machine could themselves be encoded as symbols on its tape. A machine, therefore, could read the description of another machine and simulate its behavior. This was the universal machine.

In that moment, the idea of the general computer crystallized. A single device, given the appropriate symbolic description, could perform any computation that any other machine could carry out. Programs were no longer external to machines but could be treated as data, interchangeable and interpretable.

This insight did not arise in isolation. It reflected a broader cultural shift in which modern institutions - bureaucracies, communication networks, industrial systems - increasingly organized themselves around formal procedures and encoded information. The telegraph reduced messages to signals; administration reduced governance to standardized processes. Modernity itself was becoming algorithmic.

Turing's universal machine was therefore both a mathematical discovery and a cultural synthesis. It united Leibniz's dream of calculable reason, Babbage's vision of programmable mechanism, Hilbert's demand for formal procedure, and Gödel's encoding of logic into symbol. In defining the general computer, Turing did not merely answer a technical question. He gave precise form to a possibility that had long haunted the modern imagination: that thought might be enacted by a machine, and that reasoning itself might be simulated by the manipulation of symbols.
